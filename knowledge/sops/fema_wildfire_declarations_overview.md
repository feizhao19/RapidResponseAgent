# Wildfires and Declarations (FEMA overview)

## Fire Management Assistance Grant (FMAG)

The Fire Management Assistance Grant (FMAG) program is FEMA’s primary method to assist state, local, tribal, and territorial (SLTT) governments responding to wildfires.

Under FMAG, FEMA provides grants to SLTT jurisdictions for the mitigation, management, and control of any fire on public or private forest land or grassland that threatens such destruction as would constitute a major disaster.

Even when an FMAG is approved, reimbursement generally requires the state or territory to demonstrate that the fire meets the applicable fire cost threshold for the calendar year.

## When FMAG may not be enough

FEMA generally does not recommend an emergency or major disaster declaration for a wildfire unless impacts and costs go beyond what FMAG can address.

Example: Direct Federal Assistance (DFA) is not currently authorized for FMAG declarations. A state or territory may therefore request an emergency or major disaster declaration to obtain DFA needed to save lives, protect property, maintain public health and safety, or lessen or avert the threat of a catastrophe.

## Emergency and major disaster declarations

The President can declare an emergency when federal assistance is needed to supplement SLTT efforts to:

- save lives;
- protect property and public health and safety; or
- lessen or avert the threat of a catastrophe.

Major disaster declarations support a broader recovery package when damage is of sufficient severity and magnitude.

## Operational takeaway for analysts

- Early wildfire response funding conversations often start with **FMAG**.
- If life-safety missions need Direct Federal Assistance or broader recovery tools, planners should evaluate an **emergency / major disaster** request path.
- Always verify current thresholds and eligibility with the latest FEMA guidance for the declaration date.
